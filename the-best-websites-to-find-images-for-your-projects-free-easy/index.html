The Best Websites to Find Images for Your Projects (Free & Easy)

20 March 2026

Whether you’re spicing up your Mac desktop with custom folder icons, building out a WordPress site, or putting together a mood board, finding the right image can make or break the final look. The good news is you don’t need to take your own photos or pay for expensive stock libraries. Here are the best websites to find images — free, legal, and actually good looking.

What to Look for in an Image Source

Before diving in, a quick note on image rights. Not every image you find online is free to use. Always check the license before downloading. The ones listed below are either fully free, creative commons licensed, or have a free tier that covers personal and commercial use.

1. Unsplash unsplash.com

Unsplash is the go-to for high quality, aesthetic photography. The images are contributed by photographers worldwide and are free to use for almost anything — no attribution required. The library leans toward lifestyle, nature, architecture, and tech photography.

Best for: Blog headers, website backgrounds, mood boards

2. Pexels pexels.com

Similar to Unsplash but with a slightly broader range of styles, including more diverse and everyday subjects. Also completely free with no attribution required. Pexels also has a library of free stock videos if you ever need those.

Best for: Blog posts, social media graphics, presentations

3. Pinterest pinterest.com

Pinterest is less of an image source and more of a discovery and inspiration tool. You can find incredible visual references for almost any aesthetic — minimalist Mac setups, color palettes, folder icon designs, UI inspiration, and more. Just keep in mind that most images on Pinterest link back to other sites and are not free to download and reuse. Use it to find a direction, then source the actual image from a proper free library.

Best for: Mood boarding, finding a visual style, color inspiration

4. Flaticon flaticon.com

If you’re looking for icons specifically — like custom folder icons for your Mac — Flaticon is the best resource available. It has millions of icons in every style imaginable, from flat and minimal to detailed and colorful. The free tier requires attribution, and a paid plan removes that requirement.

Best for: Custom Mac folder icons, app icons, UI elements

5. Icons8 icons8.com

Icons8 offers icons, illustrations, and photos all in one place. It’s particularly useful if you want a consistent visual style across multiple assets — everything in their library is designed to match. They also have a tool called Lunacy for editing assets directly.

Best for: Folder icons, consistent icon sets, illustrations

6. Pixabay pixabay.com

Pixabay covers photos, illustrations, vectors, and even music. The quality varies more than Unsplash or Pexels, but the sheer volume means you can almost always find what you’re looking for. All content is free under their own license.

Best for: When you need something specific and can’t find it elsewhere

7. Reshot reshot.com

Reshot is a smaller, curated library of free photos and icons. Because it’s more selective, the overall quality is consistently high. Great for finding images that don’t look like typical stock photography.

Best for: Unique, non-generic looking photos

8. macOSicons macosicons.com

This one is Mac-specific. If you’re customizing your Mac folder and app icons, macOSicons is a community-built library of thousands of free macOS-style icons — designed specifically to match the look and feel of macOS. You can search by app name or browse by category.

Best for: Custom Mac app and folder icons that actually look native

Quick Comparison

Tips for Finding the Right Image

  • Search with adjectives — instead of just “folder,” try “minimal folder icon” or “pastel folder aesthetic”
  • Filter by color — most of these platforms let you filter results by color, which is perfect if you’re trying to match a specific palette on your Mac or website
  • Download in the right format — for Mac icons, look for PNG files with transparent backgrounds. For websites, JPEG or WebP is usually best for photos
  • Stay consistent — if you’re customizing multiple folders or building a site, try to pull all your images from the same source so the style stays cohesive

Final Thoughts

You don’t need a budget or a camera to find great images. Between Unsplash, Pexels, Flaticon, and macOSicons, you have access to more high quality visuals than you could ever use — all for free. Whether you’re hunting for the perfect folder icon to match your Mac color scheme or looking for a header image for your latest blog post, one of these sites will have exactly what you need.
